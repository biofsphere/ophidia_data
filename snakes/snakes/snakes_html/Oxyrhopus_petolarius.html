DiagnosisDiagnosis (genus): see Cunha & Nascimenta 1983 (in Portuguese) Diagnosis (genus): The species of Oxyrhopus share an undivided cloacal scale, paired subcaudals, a temporal formulae of 1+2 or 2+3, 7-10 infralabials and 7-8 supralabials, as well as a deeply bilobed, bicalyculate and bicapitated hemipenis, also presenting enlarged and well-developed lateral spines, in three or four rows, usually with a nude area in the lateral region of each lobe (Peters & Orejas-Miranda 1970, Zaher 1999); these characteristics, however, are not exclusive to Oxyrhopus, and the genus remains without a reliable morphological diagnosis (Gonzales et al. 2020).

Diagnosis (digitalis): see Cunha & Nascimenta 1983: 14 (in Portuguese)

Diagnosis/Similar Species: Oxyrhopus petola can be distinguished from all other Honduran snakes in having smooth dorsal scales in 19-19-17 rows on the body and a dorsal pattern of red, black, and yellow or red and black body bands that do not cross the venter. (McCranie 2011)

Description. The following is based on six males (MCZ R-32195; SMF 86045; 1446/6; USNM 24533, 559687, 565574) and 11 females (AMNH 70211; MCZ R-31655; SMF 86044; USNM 559686, 559722, 561970, 563335-36, 564136, 565573, 565575). Oxyrhopus petola is a moderate-sized, crossbanded snake (maximum recorded TOL 1200 mm [Savage, 2002]; 1015 mm TOL in largest Honduran specimen [USNM 561970]) with a relatively short tail; head distinct from neck; snout moderately elongate, broadly rounded in dorsal outline; rostral not extending posteriorly between internasals, its length from above about one-half that of median internasal suture; internasals short, slightly over one-half length of prefrontal suture; prefrontals long, their median suture about one-half length of frontal; frontal longer than wide, widest anteriorly, length nearly equal to that of parietals, in contact with prefrontals, upper preoculars, supraoculars, and parietals; supraoculars longer than wide, shorter than frontal; parietals long, wide, their median suture shorter than frontal, parietals widest anteriorly; parietals in contact with supraoculars, frontal, upper postocular, upper anterior and upper secondary temporals, and about 6-7 nuchal scales; nasal divided, prenasal about same size as, to slightly larger than, postnasal, their combined lengths slightly longer than loreal; postnasal in contact with loreal, first and second supralabials, internasal, and prefrontal; loreal single, longer than high, length less than one-third that of snout, about two-thirds length of eye; preoculars 1-2 (usually 1); postoculars 2, both about equal in size, or upper larger than smaller; temporals usually 2+3, lower anterior one usually above sixth and seventh supralabials, lower secondary one usually above eighth supralabial,; supralabials 7-9 (usually 8), with fourth and fifth (or rarely fifth and sixth or third and fourth) bordering eye; pupil vertically elliptical; infralabials 9-11 (usually 10), first pair in contact medially posterior to mental, first 4 or 5 in contact with anterior chinshields; anterior chinshields paired, longer than wide, longer than posterior chinshields; posterior chinshields paired, separated for most of their length by 1 or 2 gulars; mental groove present; ventral 186-214 (201.2 +/- 9.7) in males [186-218 range-wide], 198-214 (202.8 +/- 4.9) in females [191-222 range-wide]; cloacal scute entire; subcaudals 84-104 (96.5 +/- 7.0) in males [84-126 range-wide], 83-99 (93.5 +/-6.0) in ten females [77-110 range-wide], divided; dorsal scales smooth, in 19-19-17 rows on body, with paired apical pits (at least on nape), without supracloacal ridges; TOL 636-1003 (843.6 +/- 133.8) mm in five males, 403-1015 (640.8 +/- 206.5) in ten females; SVL 491-752 (626.5 +/- 88.3) mm in males, 316-780 (490.4 +/- 152.9) mm in ten females; TAL/TOL 0.228-0.396 in five males, 0.191-0.256 in ten females. (McCranie 2011)

Nomenclature: Smith et al. (1986) chose O. petolarius as the correct name. See Bailey (1986) for a contrary view. This matter hinges on whether the subsequent spellings of pethola and petalarius by LinnÃ© (1766) and Gmelin (1788) are unjustified emendations or incorrect subsequent spellings (ICZN article 35). If the former, the conclusion of Smith et al. (1986) prevails; if the latter is correct then Bailey's interpretation is valid. [from SAVAGE 2002: 576].

Type species: Coluber Petola LINNAEUS 1758 is the type species of the genus Oxyrhopus WAGLER 1830 (designated by COPE 1860: 185).

Synonymy after PETERS & OREJAS-MIRANDA 1970. McCranie 2011 listed O. p. sebae as a synonym of P. petola (but did not discuss the validity outside Honduras).

Subspecies after Bailey 1970. LYNCH (2009) refused to accept subspecies because the âuse of subspecies names implies more than we knowâ.

Appearance: Oxyrhopus petola mimicks Micrurus diastema and M. limbatus. Oxyrhopus petola may be so completely melanistic that inexperienced persons confuse them with adult Clelia clelia (which does not exhibit preocular-frontal contact).

Key: Gonzales et al. 2020 present a key to the Oxyrhopus species of the central Andes. 
EtymologyThe specific name petola may be derived from the Greek petalos (broad) in reference to the relatively broad red bands found in this species (McCranie 2011: 360).

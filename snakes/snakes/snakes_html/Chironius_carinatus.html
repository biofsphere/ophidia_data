DiagnosisDiagnosis (genus): The genus is easily diagnosed as it is the only Neotropical snake genus with 12 or 10 rows of dorsal scales at midbody. All species of Chironius are diurnal arboreal snakes inhabiting preferably lowland tropical rainforests, but being also found in humid montane forests as well as open-formation biomes (Dixon et al., 1993; Marques et al., 2001; Marques and Sazima, 2003). Klaczko et al. (2014) confirmed the monophyly of Chironius despite contrary results obtained by Pyron et al. (2013).

Diagnosis: Dixon et al. 1993: 68 ff (incl. carinatus, spixi) 
CommentSynonymy partly after PETERS & OREJAS-MIRANDA 1970. The photo of Chironius carinatus in VILLA et al. (1988) is not a Ch. carinatus according to SCOTT 1989: 803.

Type species: Coluber carinatus LINNAEUS 1758 is the type species of the genus Chironius FITZINGER 1826.

Subspecies: Ch. carinatus spixi is the only taxon in Chironius that does not undergo an ontogenetic change in eye size, perhaps because it is also the only Chironius species that does not spend the majority of its time in closed-canopy forests (DIXON et al. 1993). Ch. carinatus spixi intergrades with carinatus carinatus in Venezuela (Dixon et al., 1993: 69, 72).

Distribution: Map: Dixon et al. 1993: 69 (incl. subspecies). BMNH 53.9.26.25 from Guadeloupe, USNM 12136 from Paraguay, and SMNS 5390 from Argentina all represent erroneous locality data (fide DIXON et al. 1993: 74). Reports from Central America (e.g. from Costa Rica, Solorzano 2004) correspond to other species in the genus. Not in Ecuador fide Torres-Carvajal et al. 2019: 299 ff). See map in Nogueira et al. 2019.

Phylogeny: see Hamdan et al. 2017.

Habitat: partly arboreal (Harrington et al. 2018).

Diet: most Chironius feed on reptiles, birds, and/or mammals (Roberto et al. 2020). 
EtymologyNamed after Latin “carinatus” = keeled (like a ship).

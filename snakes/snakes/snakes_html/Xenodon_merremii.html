Reproductionoviparous 
TypesHolotype: lost, was ZSM, uncatalogued specimen(s) (lost fide Franzen & Glaw 2007), collected by Spix and Martius expedition to Brazil, 1817-1820. 
DiagnosisDescription: The head is robust, well differentiated from the rest of the body. 2 internasals and 2 prefrontals. One preocular and 1 or 2 postoculars; 7 supralabials (third and fourth in contact with the eye); 9-11 infralabials, the fifth in contact with the first geneial which is about twice as long as the second. Loreal present in contact with second and third supralabials. Temporals 1+2 or 1+3. Hefty body. Dorsal scales smooth, without pits, in 19-19-17 fils (exceptionally 19-19-15). Ventrals 133-147 (131-150 according to Giraudo, 2001). Subcaudals divided 37-48 in males; 32-40 in females (30-36 according to Giraudo, 2001). Whole or divided anal. Divided hemipenis; spermatic groove divided midway from body; presence of apical discs; body and base of the lobes covered by moderate spines arranged homogeneously although somewhat larger on the sides and closer to the base. (translated from Carreira et al. 2005: 384).

Size: total length to more than 1 m, although most specimens are smaller. Giraudo (2001) mentions a female specimen of 1275 mm. The young measure at birth between 160 and 193 mm with a weight of 3.3-5.8g (Pontes & Di-Bernardo, 1988). The tail is generally longer in males, representing a fifth of total length, one eighth in females. (translated from Carreira et al. 2005: 384).

Coloration: the general coloration is in shades of chestnut (exceptionally grey), on a variable background in shades of olive, cream and yellow. It presents a pattern of dark brown dorsal spots, edged with light (white), variable in form. They can appear as wide irregular bands, X-shaped, and even in some specimens with an appearance similar to the design observed in Bothrops alternatus. These bands continue even in the caudal region. On the head, three (rarely two) oblique dark bands are seen, one continuing behind of the eye (postocular), another parallel to the previous one that is born in the parietal region and last a subsequent one that extends from the neck. It is possible that in some individuals these bands are less recognizable and that in old specimens the entire dorsal and ventral body design looks very diffuse. Ventrally, on whitish background, irregular dark blotches are present on the edges of the gastrostegos, which can be extended to a greater or lesser extent over the region caudal (some specimens have the caudal region immaculate). This ventral pattern is also similar to that of Bothrops alternatus, with the difference that in the latter species the ventral spots form angles (in X. merremi they are rounded). Juveniles have the same coloration as adults. (translated from Carreira et al. 2005: 385).

The color pattern of this species is quite variable and includes leucism and melanistic specimens (see photos).

Other descriptions: Cei (1986), Cei (1993), Giraudo (2001), Boulenger 1894: 150. 
CommentSynonymy after CEI 1993.

Type species: Ophis Merremii WAGLER 1824 is the type species of the genus Waglerophis ROMANO & HOGE 1972. However, Waglerophis was synonymized with Xenodon by ZAHER et al. 2009. 
EtymologyNamed after Blasius Merrem (1761-1824), German zoologist, and Johann Georg Wagler (1800-1832), German physician and zoologist.
